# Creating Your World

A game of Rulers has to happen somewhere, right? In this next section we will walk you through our method of randomly creating your world. You may already have ideas on what you want the world to look like. Perhaps it's a very magical world, Arabian themed or in a certain period of history, but no matter what you have in mind, we believe a randomly generated world can improve it and give your initial idea more variety and depth. The map our rules will generate is a guideline, a suggestion for you to piece together into the setting you want for your game. Think of it as randomly generated inspiration!

We are going to roll dice on a series of tables to create a rough idea of what our world looks like. We'll roll everything before beginning to sketch out what your world will look like.

**What you will need:**
- Paper & Pencil
- d10s, d8s

**A finished world should have:**
- A Map
- Territories
- Resources
- A Narrative Setting

## Step 1: Creating Climates

The first step is to have an idea of what the climate is like in different areas on our map. Is it hot or cold, dry or wet? What does the landscape look like and what plants and animals might live there?

Roll a d10 on the climate table 8 times. For every roll, also roll a d8 on the location table to determine its rough location. Multiple rolls on the same location are allowed.

**Location Table**

| Roll (1d8) | Location |
|---|---|
| 1 | North |
| 2 | North East |
| 3 | East |
| 4 | South East |
| 5 | South |
| 6 | South West |
| 7 | West |
| 8 | North West |

**Climate Table**

| Roll (2d10) | Climate |
|---|---|
| 2–3 | Desert |
| 4–6 | Savannah |
| 7–8 | Mediterranean |
| 9–10 | Steppes |
| 11 | Oceanic |
| 12–13 | Forested |
| 14–15 | Alpine |
| 16–18 | Tropical |
| 19–20 | Tundra |

### Climate Descriptions

**Desert:** Deserts are any area which receives very little or almost no rain. However, there can be torrential annual rains. They are usually inland, and are often flanked by Mountains, Steppes or Savannahs. *Examples: Sahara Desert, Gobi Desert.*

**Savannah:** Savannahs are arid grasslands, with tall dry grasses and few, sparsely leaved trees. They can still support an abundance of life though, which has evolved to survive in this climate, like gazelle, cheetahs and elephants. Wild fires are always a risk. They are usually the border between Forested, or wetter areas, and Deserts or very dry areas.

**Mediterranean:** A Mediterranean climate has hot, dry summers, but relatively cool winters, with powerful storms in the autumn. Its ground is arid and often has craggy rocks jutting from its surface. Its plants have thin, oily leaves to conserve water loss. They are usually found on the coast, and often border between slightly wetter and slightly dryer climates. *Examples: Greece, Italy, Spain, California, Morocco.*

**Steppes:** Steppes are often large areas of grasslands. There are few shrubs, and no trees, only short grasses. Their summers are cool and their winters can be freezing. The winds are strong and often constant. Animals are few and far between, and are often grazers like horses, yaks and sheep. They are usually found next to mountains, or dryer areas like deserts. *Examples: Mongolian Steppes, American Prairie.*

**Oceanic:** Rain rolls over the windy hills in these areas. Vegetation is often lush, with copious amounts of thick grasses, bushes and deciduous trees. Animals of all kinds live in these areas. The summers can be pleasant. They are usually found on the coast, or near wet, Forested areas. *Examples: The UK, The Netherlands, Nova Scotia.*

**Forested:** Trees abound in a Forested area. If they are near wet areas they are likely to be tangled clusters of deciduous trees, but if it's near a dry area, they may instead be dense rows of coniferous trees. Perhaps even both. Forested areas can exist in either dry or wet areas, but they are usually close to other areas with high vegetation, like Oceanic, Alpine or Tropical areas. They can also form a border between these areas and places with less vegetation, like Steppes, Savannah and Mediterranean areas.

**Alpine:** Alpine areas are usually on mountains, very close to them, or at least in areas of high elevation. They are often quite cold and snowy in the winter, but still have moderately warm summers. All year round they are relatively dry. Plantlife often has thin leaves to conserve water. All trees are coniferous. They nearly always border on mountains, and often border on other dry areas.

**Tropical:** Tropical areas have humid climates and dense, deciduous trees and undergrowth. Life is abundant in these areas, and not always friendly. They are often warm all year round, and rainstorms are frequent in the hot summer months. They can be found on coastal areas, or inland. They are usually adjacent to other hot or dry climates, and often border other densely forested areas.

**Tundra:** These dry and icy climates are devoid of nearly all vegetation, except for the occasional secluded pine forest. Even in the summers there is still little to no rainfall, although rivers surge down valleys as the ice and snow melts. Tundra are usually adjacent to other dry areas and sparsely vegetated areas, often flanked by mountains.

## Step 2: Making Mountains

Mountain ranges will shape our world. They divide climates and provide valleys for rivers. Mountains trap rainfall, and often border dry areas.

Roll 1d3+1 (halve the value on a d6) to determine how many mountain ranges there are in the world. For each mountain range, roll on the Location Table once. It's up to the GM to decide how long each mountain range is and how many mountains are in each.

## Step 3: Coasts and Rivers

Water is not only essential to life, but also to civilisation. Rivers, lakes and seas allow civilisations to move people, ideas and trade over large distances with ease.

Roll on the 'Rivers & Seas' table 6 times. For each roll, roll on the Location Table once. If a River is rolled, roll on the Location Table twice to determine where the River starts and where it ends.

**Rivers & Seas Table**

| Roll (1d10) | Result |
|---|---|
| 1 | Lake |
| 2–4 | River |
| 5–6 | Estuary |
| 7–9 | Coast |
| 10 | Island |

## Putting it all Together

Now to make our world! Take a sheet of paper and a pencil and let's get sketching. The relative size of the map is up to you. It can be the whole world, a continent or an island. Feel free to make more maps to detail the whole globe your game exists on.

Make a note of what climates are where, and consider what the terrain will have to be like to create them (Mountains cut off dry flat basins, estuaries will connect to rivers and wet areas, etc.). Positioning is another thing to keep in mind. If you have something in the "North", that can be anywhere within a cone extending from just above the centre of your map. If you have two locations that are the same, one will be closer to the centre than the other.

### Phase 1: Coastlines

We'll start with the outline of your landmass. Sketch out a length of coastline in each location where you rolled one. Remember, coastlines are jagged, and are made of peninsulas, estuaries, bays and often have small rocky islands. What you've rolled details significant features, but feel free to throw in smaller ones where you think appropriate.

### Phase 2: Mountains

Mountains will form walls around our climates and begin to shape our world dramatically. Anywhere there is a dry Climate, it needs to be cut off from the sea by mountains. Start with the driest climates you have. They don't need to be completely walled in, just placed as a barrier between them and the sea.

**Dry Climates (driest to least dry):** Desert = Tundra > Steppes = Savannah > Alpine = Mediterranean

### Phase 3: Rivers

All rivers end at the ocean, and they all start in mountains or lakes. While you have a start and end location for your river, it will not move in a straight line. Snake-wind down from higher land, cut valleys through the mountains. Rivers form good borders between wetter and drier climates, and help to explain where the sudden increase in moisture came from. Keep in mind that rivers will allow the people of each Realm to travel faster by sailing down them, so try to cover a lot of the map.

### Phase 4: Climates

Now that seas, mountains and rivers explain the borders of our climates, we can start filling them in. Put your driest climates by their mountain walls, and slightly less dry climates adjacent to them. If you have densely Forested areas, they are likely to be near other Climates dense in vegetation, like Alpine, Tropical and Oceanic areas. Although Savannah and Mediterranean Climates are not devoid of trees entirely.

Consider which way the equator lies. Perhaps it's to the south or possibly the east? Put the warmer climates, like the Mediterranean and Tropical climates closer to it, with the Alpine and Tundra areas as far away from it as possible.

### Phase 5: Colour it in!

Let's face it, this is the best part. Now that you've sketched out your map, you can draw it up nicely, with some details and colour! I personally suggest just adding a hint of colour along the border of climates, and having a simple key to keep to. As long as your players know what your symbol for "Tree" is, you can leave a lot of the work to their imagination.

If you can, scan it in and edit it on your computer. I really recommend doing this when adding the players, NPCs and their cities. You don't want to mess up your nice map of the world just because you can't spell "The Kingdom of Ferlintoquzeres".
