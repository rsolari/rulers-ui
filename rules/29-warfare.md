# Warfare

Wars can be fought to protect the Realm, or to expand it, but like any war, it is down to the soldiers on the frontline to interpret their orders as best they can. Each Troop is led by a Knight, and any collective group of Troops form an Army, which must itself be led by a Noble. This Noble is then considered a General. A General is given orders by their Ruler (which they may or may not follow).

When an Army attempts to fulfil its orders (quelling a rebellion, routing bandits, defending a pass, capturing a city etc.) then it is up to the N.O. to decide what level of combat needs to be played out.

There are three levels of combat: **Automatic**, **Quick Resolve**, and **Full Combat**.

- In **Automatic**, the outcome of the battle is decided by the N.O. with agreement from all players involved.
- For **Quick Resolve**, a small dice roll is made to determine the outcome.
- In **Full Combat**, the whole battle is roleplayed by all the parties involved, with multiple dice rolls.

The rule of thumb for the N.O. is to decide: *"Is this battle worth playing out?"* It is important that the N.O. makes a decision that adds to the fun of the game, and knows when to deal with battles quickly, but also when to allow a player to come back through roleplaying.

---

## Battles — Automatic

> *A thousand Elite Amethysian "Harlequins", accompanied by a dozen of their best cannon, assemble near the gates of a small Mordravale border town, with only a couple of Archers to defend it. You can guess who wins.*

This is an example where an Automatic battle might be fought. One side has overwhelming force, and even though their goal is significant (the capture of a town), there really is no hope for the defending forces to come out on top. The N.O. therefore rules that the Amethysian victory is automatic.

If however, the defenders had a battalion of the legendary Onor'Tok on their side, the N.O. may decide it is worth seeing how the battle plays out. A memorable story of a gallant last stand might at least happen, and they might at least reduce the numbers of the attacking Army.

## Battles — Quick Combat

> *A number of Shadowfox Light Cavalrymen are sent to scout a Bandit camp, which has been launching attacks against trade wagons passing through a nearby valley. When they get there, they are ambushed by a smattering of crossbowmen and thugs. This battle should be decided quickly, but it's an even fight.*

Quick Resolve is used to decide battles where the outcome is less certain, but in the interest of time, are not worth playing out in Full Combat.

Each Army (or simple collection of Troops) will roll their total dice pool against each other. The side with the most successful rolls will be the victor.

- For every Basic Troop, roll 1 dice. A roll of 5–6 is a success.
- For every Elite Troop, roll 1 dice, where a roll of 4–6 is a success.
- Keep track of which dice are rolling for what class of Troop.
- For every 3 units of the same Troop type, grant an additional +1 dice.
- Then add dice for bonuses. This bonus is only granted once, but multiple instances of the same bonus stack.

**Example:** Spearmen grant a bonus +1 dice if they are against Cavalry units, while Pikemen have a bonus of +2 dice. Together they would give a bonus of +3 dice against an army which has any Cavalry troops. However, multiple Spearmen or Pikemen Troops would not increase this bonus further.

If both belligerents have more than 10 dice in their pool, only roll the difference in dice pools, in the interests of dice rolling sanity. For instance, if the total dice pool of two opposing forces are 13 and 15, simply roll 3 dice against 5. In this case, Elite Troops always carry over to the dice being rolled.

## Battles — Full Combat

> *The Armies of Mordravale descend on the Amethysian capital. Their forces have amassed near the banks of a river. The rainfall is heavy and the Amethysians are unable to rely on their gunpowder; they will instead have to hope the Onor'Tok can reach the devastating Mordravale Archers before the battle is lost. This fight matters.*

Full Combat is used to resolve battles where both the N.O. and the players feel it worth playing out the whole battle. This might be because it is a significant story event in the game, because there is a lot at stake, or both.

Each Army has a moment to describe its position on the Battlefield, with the Defenders going first. If an Army has been ambushed, the defenders describe what their likely marching formation would have been. When determining the position of Troops, simply detail which ones are in the Centre, and on the Left and Right Flanks. If they were travelling in a column, the Troops may be in the Front, Middle or Back. The Battlefield is to be described by the N.O. and should largely be dictated by the Climate the belligerents are in.

### Troop Actions & Initiative

Once the scene of the battle has been set, the Defenders are given the initiative to act first. This process is largely descriptive, and Troops may act as is reasonable for them to do so. Each Troop may **Move**, **Act** or **Ready** an action on their turn. Depending on the Armour or Weapon Type of the Troop, they may be able to Move and Act on their turn.[^34] Once all of the Defenders' Troops have acted, it is then the turn of the Attackers, and so on.

### Resolving Dice Rolls

To resolve combat between two units of Troops, determine their total dice pools and roll them against each other.

- Basic Troops start with a pool of: **4d6**
- Elite Troops start with a pool of: **6d6**

Remove or add dice depending on the Troop's Bonuses and Weaknesses, and any other effects which the N.O. feels are relevant. The N.O. may also add bonus dice for good storytelling and strategic reasoning.

Multiple Troops of the same type, who are fighting multiple Troops who are also of a matching type, only need one dice roll to resolve the combat. Essentially, it doesn't matter if it's 100 Spearmen against 100 Cavalrymen, or 10,000 Spearmen against 10,000 Cavalrymen — the result is still basically the same. The N.O. may however award bonus dice if one side outnumbers the other.

Troops who are not being attacked themselves cannot suffer the negative effects of losing their Combat Roll, even though they can still fail to take the Advantage in battle (e.g. Archers firing from a wall).[^35]

Count the number of net successes and see which unit came out with an Advantage:

| Advantage | Consequence |
|---|---|
| +1–2 | Wounded |
| +3–4 | Routed |
| +5 | Defeated |
| +6 or more | Crushed |

### Wounding Troops

A Troop exists in one of five conditions: **Healthy**, **Wounded**, **Routed**, **Defeated** and **Crushed**.

Healthy Troops act normally, but as they are wounded they increasingly see the benefits in fleeing or surrendering. Every combat roll any Troop makes against another will result in some mental and physical trauma.

**Wounded:** A Wounded Troop has −1 Dice to all of its dice rolls (e.g. Combat, Morale). The Troop can still Move and Act normally. Each successive time a Troop is Wounded, it advances one step down the Wound Table.

**Routed:** A Routed Troop must pass a Morale save or flee the battlefield. The Troop rolls its base dice pool, plus or minus any modifiers to Morale or dice rolls, against the number of Successes which were rolled against it (3 or 4, unless altered by modifiers). If it succeeds on its Morale save, then the Troop stays on the Battlefield, but suffers −2 Dice to all of its future dice rolls. If it is Routed again but still remains on the battlefield, it advances one step down the Wound table.

| Wound Table | Effect |
|---|---|
| Wounded 1 | −1 Dice |
| Wounded 2 | −1 Dice |
| Routed 1 | −2 Dice |
| Routed 2 | −2 Dice |
| Defeated | No Dice rolls |

**Defeated:** Defeated Troops are either critically wounded, have fled the battle, or have surrendered. However it is described, they are removed from the battle and no longer participate.

**Crushed:** If a Troop is Crushed, it is definitely dead. Very, very dead. Not only does it no longer participate in the battle, it decorates the castle walls with its entrails. A troop can only be Crushed by a direct roll — otherwise it is Defeated.

Each successive time a Troop loses a Combat Roll but remains on the battlefield, it advances one stage down the Wound Table. Therefore if a Troop has already passed two Morale Saves, the third time it is Routed it will become Defeated no matter what.

If the result of the Combat rolls exceeds Wounding and applies one of the other consequences of losing a combat roll, jump to that position on the Wound Table.

### Combat Example

A Troop of Mordravale Swordsmen charge the Amethysian gunline. The Swordsmen spend their action charging. It is now the Amethysians' turn to act, and they fire upon the charging Mords.

The Amethysian Harlequins are fresh and have received no Wounds; they roll 4 Dice +1 additional dice (for a total of 5d6), because their weapons give them an advantage against Armoured Troops.

The Mord Swordsmen have already been Wounded twice by the Amethysian Harlequins, so the Mord Swordsmen are at Wound 2 (−1 Dice). However, they are Elite Troops (6d6), so they roll 5d6 to defend.

The Amethysians roll 4, 1, 5, 6, 2. The 5 and the 6 are successes. The Mords roll 3, 5, 4, 2, 2. Only the 5 is a success.

The Amethysians win the combat roll by 1 Success. Normally, this would only Wound a Troop, but because the Mord Troop is already at Wound 2, they advance into Routed. They must now make a Morale roll to stay on the battlefield.

Mord Nobles have a proud Chivalric Tradition, and so their Elite have a +1 to Morale rolls, but they are now at −2 Dice because they are at Routed 1. They will roll 5d6 (6d6 − 2 + 1 = 5d6) against the 1 Success from the Amethysian Harlequins.

The Mord Swordsmen roll 1, 5, 3, 4, 4. They pass the Morale roll just barely and with a tremendous roar they continue to charge through the hail of lead shot.

They crash into the Harlequins in a flurry of steel. They are at −2 for Routed 1, but gain a +1 bonus against Light Troops. They roll 5d6 for 3 successes (4, 6, 5, 6, 4) while the Amethysians roll 4d6 to defend (3, 2, 1, 2) and have no successes.

The gunline is Routed, and now must pass a Morale save. They have yet to be wounded, so they roll their base dice pool of 4d6; they need 3 successes. They roll 2, 6, 3 for 1 success. The Harlequins have failed their Morale check and are Routed! Against the terrifying might of the Mord swordsmen, they begin to flee for their lives.

In Full Combat, the battle is over when one side concedes defeat, or if all of an Army's Troops have been Routed or Defeated. Alternatively, the Players and the N.O. can agree on an objective that decides the battle, such as the capture of a Gatehouse, or the defeat of the General.

[^34]: This is largely the case for Melee troops, but not Ranged ones.
[^35]: Archers firing from a Settlement Wall against an entirely Melee Army cannot be Wounded. However, the N.O. may deem it appropriate for them to be Routed if, say, the Gate is breached and the Archers are about to be overrun.
