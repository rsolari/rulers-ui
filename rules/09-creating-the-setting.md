# Creating the Setting

Now that we have world, a map, resources and we know where our players
and NPCs are, it is time to create a setting. The setting determines the
overall narrative of our game, and provides much needed context to the
world our players will be interacting with. Creating a Shared history
Discuss ideas with your players. Find out what their con - cepts are,
and start to create a shared history. Do people have similar religion,
or races? If so, why are they not part of the same Realm ? Was one Realm
found - ed by escaped slaves/criminals from another? How were the
borders shaped? Who is the oldest Realm , and who is the youngest?\
The Narrative Context A good setting should have an implied storyline
from its context. What are the goals of the players and NPCs? Why are
they here? What is at stake?\
From our playtests, we found that this question was the key component to
creating a good setting: "Why do I need to change the Status Quo?" If
your players do not feel a need to shape the world around them, then
they will simply not interact with it in any meaningful way. Force your
players to take sides, act quickly or ally together. While they will
certainly have their objectives, give the scenario something the players
must react to.\
Here are some example concepts you can use for your game, or to give you
inspiration. Setting Suggestions: The Fallen Empire Once all of the
Rulers were united, either by common interest, or perhaps just by force,
but now the Emperor has died, possibly even assassinated. Three factions
have emerged who want to change the order of the world. One faction
thinks that the old system was flawed, but that it can be improved. They
want to elect a new Em - peror from one of the existing Rulers. Another
faction wants to seize the opportunity to dissolve the Empire, and grant
independence to all the Rulers . While a third remains loyal to old
Empire, and is trying to find a suit - able heir to the throne. Perhaps
the Emperor had no heir, or maybe his heir is only an infant child.Give
each faction a name, and an NPC leading it. To fur - ther split opinion,
make sure each faction has different resources at its disposal. "Why do
I need to change the Status Quo?" Because civil war threatens the
security of my Realm, and if I do not have a say in what the new order
will be, it may not be to my liking. Inspiration Japan: In 1598 Toyotomi
Hideyoshi died, leaving his infant son, Hideyori, to inherit his rule. A
council of re - agents were supposed to govern Japan until the boy was
old enough, but the Daimyo lords seized the opportunity to take power
for themselves. After the decisive battle of Sekigahara in 1600,
Tokugawa Ieyasu secured his position as the Shogun of Japan, and his
family would rule until the Meiji Restoration in 1868, which finally
dissolved the feudal system. Is independence best, or are there
advantages to an Empire? Who will be the new Emperor, a player, an NPC,
or a puppet? A Brave New World All of our players are small lords of a
much larger king - dom or empire. They have come to colonise a new
world, supposedly rich in resources. However, they have not found it
entirely uninhabited. When creating your regions, consider having twice
as many neutral ones. Keep in mind though that the locals won't
recognise these borders in the same way as these foreigner visitors do.
Alternatively, instead of additional neutral territories, have a large
disparate Empire take up most of the map. "Why do I need to change the
Status Quo?" Because everyone is going to expand rapidly in this new
world, and I will pushed away from the best spots if I don't act quickly

coming. An apocalyptic event awaits our players and they must prepare

Thermopylae: In Ancient Greece, there was no such thing as one united
body. Instead there were lots of small city-states. The most powerful of
which was Athens, and its biggest rival was the city of Sparta. However,
when the Persian Empire decided to expand onto Grecian land, people did
recognise the need to ally together against this larger threat. At the
Battle of Thermopylae, an alliance of Spartans, Athens and other
city-states tried to hold back the Persian forces. They were defeated,
but it bought the Athenians some time. Athens still bore the brunt of
the war, and it was still the Athenian navy who finally sent the
Persians packing,15 but for a brief period these rival cities banded
together for the greater good of all. How long do we have?\
