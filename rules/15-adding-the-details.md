# Adding the Details

Rulers is a game of politics, economy and warfare, but more than that, it is a storytelling game about your civilisations. Once the mechanics of your Realm are in place (Resources, Traditions, Government etc.) it's time to start giving it depth.

- Who lives in your Realm?
- What is their anatomy like?
- What belief binds their society together?
- How does their language sound?
- What do they eat and drink?
- What does their clothing look like?
- What is the architectural style of the buildings?
- What is a common saying for your people?

Answering these questions, and any more you can think of, is what will allow your game of Rulers to be a rich and memorable storytelling experience.

## Race

In Rulers, the race of your Realm can be anything you imagine. They can be Elves, Orcs, Troglodytes, Demons, Centaurs, Arachnids, or Piratical Parrots. It is up to the N.O. to set the tone for the game, and set down any limitations on what kinds of fantastical races may be allowed. For instance, the N.O. may want a Human-only historical setting, or to demand that players be more creative than the usual Tolkien fantasy races.

As general guidelines, we recommend the following:

- All Races are mortal[^19]
- All Races must eat and/or drink something for sustenance
- There is an explanation for why Races can be harmed by warfare or disease

Without these basic guidelines, players may try and sneak their way out of playing by the rules.

## Faith

In Rulers we use "Faith" to refer to any system of belief that binds a society together. This can be a canonical Church, a tradition of Ancestor Worship, or even a pronounced Nationalism. While we encourage Players to create a rich and varied culture for their Realm, it is not necessary to write down every gospel and deity before the game starts. Begin by setting out a few key principles for your Faith, naming any principal characters, and establishing the manner in which people and clergy may practise this faith.

## Language

The hardest part of Rulers is coming up with names: there's all the settlements, your Ruler, every Noble, their family members, individual characters, gods, poets and inventors — there are a lot of names. That's why establishing a language is so important. You don't want to make it up as you go, because as epic as names like "Ascernon the Thirteenth of the Elgorar Elven Kings of Monsomera" may sound, no one is going to ever remember their name — including you.

Your language does not need to have a complete syntax, with every aspect of the grammar calculated. Simply begin by establishing what your language sounds like. Take one from the real world you like, and pair it with another, to avoid simply making direct translations all the time.[^20] Then, establish a naming system so that naming new Characters and Settlements follows a set of rules.

For instance, the Mord language is based on German and Swedish. Every Noble surname is "Von [Family Name]", while the peasantry are "Van [Home Settlement]". In Amethysia, every name is a regal sounding English forename, followed by a Geological period, such as Stanley Archean, or Winnifred Silurian the Third. Settlement names are often descriptive. Abertawe is the Welsh name for the city of Swansea. "Aber" means "On" and "Tawe" is the name of the River — "On-the-Tawe". So look at the language you have adopted and determine what words are for "Town", "Hill", "River", "Bridge" or "Port".

Mordravale uses a lot of Germanic names, and late Medieval-European weapons and armour. But its politics is heavily based on the Soviet Union, and its architecture is typical of Medieval Gothic. To give Amethysia a more distinct style, their clothing and architecture is inspired by the British Colonies in India and Egypt, where turbans and buttoned coats come together with tea and piles of cushions to recline upon.

## The Finer Touches

As you delve deeper and deeper into your game of Rulers, you will come face to face with questions you may not have thought of. One of my favourite questions to come up is: "What would my people say instead of: Oh my God?"

Exclamations of surprise or anger are very common to all languages. So what do your people say? This adds such an element of depth to your culture to be able to speak appropriately. The creator of the Cyrians came up with the idea of using a Saint, plus an element of clothing. The more private the garment, the more you were shocked or enraged. For example, "By Aerren's Golden Pantaloons!" expresses surprise similar to a man rushing down the street, almost knocking you over. While "By Cyrintha's Garter-Belt!" would not be acceptable to say in front of polite society.

Give your Ruler a gesture they often repeat. Come up with a popular joke in your Realm. What song can be heard echoing from the taverns this time of year? What is the most common subject of art these days? The finer touches can really make your Realm and its characters something you and your friends will remember for years to come.

## Creating an Original Society

Maybe your Realm is a race of Lizard-people who live in Samurai style castles, use Katanas and follow Bushido. That's fine, but it is simply a scalier version of Feudal Japan.[^21] But ask yourself if there is a way to add a unique spin on your idea. Are there other Realms your people would have elements of a shared culture with? Asking questions like this can take a good idea, and make it fantastic.

Games of Rulers are always better when there are powerful relationships between Realms. Is your race related to another in the world? Is your Church the last bastion of Orthodoxy, or are you heretics who escaped persecution from another Realm? Was it a shift in climate, or a great technological advance, that set you apart from your former compatriots?

Let's take another look at the Lizard-Samurai. The idea of a noble warrior elite who are masters of both the pen and the sword shares a few ideals with the Sikh religion. Now you've added a religious dimension to your Realm, which governs their dress and way of life. But why did this become the dominant faith? Perhaps our Lizard-Warriors come from a region that was on the losing end of a war? The monasteries became strongholds for desperate refugees, forcing them to take up weapons and combine faith and training to secure their survival. Now our Lizard-Warriors are a dominant military power, and consider themselves justified in retaking what was once their territory, having suffered so much to lose it. Now you're starting to create a society which blends several ideas together, which describes their place in the history of your world.
